The Process

Good questions
deserve real answers.

Most clients arrive with the same questions — and they are all worth asking. Here's what [Attorney Name] hears most often, with the kind of clear, honest answers you should have before your transaction begins.

Q.1 When should I get an attorney involved? −

Before you sign anything, if possible. The best time to call is when you have an accepted offer but haven't signed the contract yet — that's when I can do the most for you. Illinois gives buyers and sellers a 5-business-day attorney modification period after signing, and I use every day of it. But if you're earlier in the process and just want to understand what to expect, please call. That conversation is always free.

Q.2 What does a real estate attorney actually do at closing? +

A lot more than most people realize. I review and prepare closing documents, verify the title is clear, confirm the numbers match what was agreed, walk you through every page you sign, and make sure nothing unexpected came up between contract and close. Illinois law requires an attorney at every real estate closing — the question is whether yours is genuinely working for you. I always am.

Q.3 What will you look for when you review my contract? +

Everything — but especially the things buyers and sellers typically overlook: contingency language, inspection deadlines, HOA and condo association terms, what's included in the sale, and any clauses that shift risk in ways you may not expect. I explain every section in plain language and write modifications where the contract doesn't adequately protect your interests. If something's off, you'll hear about it directly.

Q.4 How do I reach you when something comes up? +

You call me. That's it. You'll have my direct number from the start, and I return calls the same day — even when I'm in a closing. Real estate transactions move quickly and questions almost never arrive at convenient moments. You shouldn't have to wonder if your attorney got your message. With me, you won't have to.

Illinois is an attorney state for real estate closings. That means you're already paying for legal representation — the question is whether that attorney is working for you or simply facilitating paperwork. [Attorney Name] represents buyers and sellers exclusively, and never both sides of the same transaction.
